Managing the 2nd Year Sleep Regression

Emma Wilson
March 15, 2026 · 8 min read

If you're reading this, you probably had a "good sleeper" who suddenly... isn't. You're not alone. The 2-year-old sleep regression is one of the most challenging developmental milestones for parents to navigate. Between 24 and 30 months, toddlers experience a massive growth spurt—not just physically, but emotionally and cognitively.

Why Now?

This is the age where "testing boundaries" becomes a full-time job. They are learning about independence, and bedtime is often the first place they choose to exert their new-found control. They start testing what happens if they keep asking for one more sip of water or a fourth bedtime story.

Common Signs to Watch For:

  • Sudden resistance to bedtime
  • Frequent night waking or calling out
  • Significantly shorter naps or refusing them altogether
  • Intense "separation anxiety" at lights out

"Regression is often a sign of progression. Your child's brain is working overtime to master new skills, and sleep temporarily takes a backseat."

Three Tips to Get Back on Track

1. **Stick to Your Routine**: Even if they are screaming, the routine provides them with a sense of security. Don't skip steps to "speed things up." Consistency is your best friend during this phase. Maintaining a predictable, calming wind-down period of 30-45 minutes helps signal to their developing brain that transitions are safe.

2. **Offer Choice, Not Control**: Instead of asking "do you want to go to bed?" (where the answer is always no), ask "do you want the blue pajamas or the yellow pajamas?" This gives them a sense of autonomy within the boundaries you set. Autonomy is the primary driver of toddler behavior, and satisfying this need early can prevent bedtime power struggles.

3. **Check Their Nap Schedule**: Many 2-year-olds need a slight adjustment to their daytime sleep. If the bridge between nap and bedtime is too short, they might not be tired enough to settle easily. Ensure at least a 5-6 hour wake window before bedtime on nap days.
